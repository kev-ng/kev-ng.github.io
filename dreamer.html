Dreamer: Web Portal for Dreams

Dreamer is a playful web portal designed for creatives to get inspiration from sharing dreams and exploring artworks from the National Library of Australia.

Client: National Library of Australia
Type: Master Course Work
Duration: 3 Months
Team Size: 5
Major Responsibilities: UXUI Design, Full-stack web development, Facilitate Design Activities, Prototype

Project context: Design Computing Studio I

The project was part of the design computing studio course delivered during the second semester of the programme. Most students had only started learning coding and design for a semester, and this was the course aimed to develop our design, web coding, and collaboration skills through a design brief of a client— the National Library of Australia.

Challenge: Designing for the Trove API

The challenge was to develop a web application for exploring contents from the Trove API - an API which gathers library and museum archives like historical images, books, newspapers, artworks and more. It was specified that we are not designing a search engine, or a new user interface for the Trove API, but rather, to design for a purpose with the use of Trove data. A short questionnaire with people around the school and an exploration of the content on our own led us to understand that the content is mostly historical, unrelated, different, and somewhat boring to people. From that we framed the challenge as a mission to create memorable interaction with the boring contents and dull experience with Trove, in specific:

How might we create a relatable, different, and engaging way for creatives to interact with Trove contents?

My involvement

As the project lead, I initiated the project by drafting and maintaining key tasks, deadlines, and responsibilities each week on a shared google sheet. I ran report back sessions and facilitated design/coding sprints. In making important decisions, I led discussions to reach agreement among the team. I organized several after class design/coding sprints with specific goals, design activities, and report sessions to make sure the team is meeting the milestones collaboratively and on schedule. Apart from the ideation and research tasks that everyone participated in, I was majorly responsible for the front-end development and interaction design for the project. I did also contribute to solving some major back-end issues as I have experience working with databases and PHP.

Designing for the content

Discovering types of content on Trove was our first step of research. I created a table of content types that I found most interesting for later reference. We then began understanding our audience by observing people’s frustrations interacting with trove. We also interviewed people about the most memorable expereinces they had with a website. Few of those were most inspirational for our design direction, for instance, I-remeber and Discover your Patronus by Pottermore. With the magical experience we aim to design for, the movie Inside Out and Harry Potter are inspirations in which we took references to. Academic HCI research related to creating a magical experience was also referenced when designing the platform.

Ideation

Based on the frustrations people had interacting with Trove contents, I came up with few ideas that were later refined and combined into the initial version of “dreamer”.

Individually we each created storyboards to refine our best concepts to pitch to the team. Based on the criteria developed from the design brief and our design direction, we voted on the best concept among the team with an explanation as to how it fits best to the project.

Analysis on similar products

There are a variety of applications for dream sharing, dream documenting, and dream exploring. In film and literature, dreams are often presented in very “magical” ways, whereas most dream-related web applications (e.g. Dreams Cloud and In My Dream) present dreams in rather unimaginative ways. These websites look more like pinboards and forums. While they may make dream sharing and dream searching easy, the application is not very sucessful in capturing the emotive and atmospheric qualities of dreams.

Paper prototyping and testing

We tested our paper prototype with people by using the Wizard of Oz method. I planned the testing session by assigning roles for each member, and preparing tasks for testing. We documented users responses with a range of methods including observation notes, video, questionnaire, and open-ended discussions.

We made various adjustments to the concept and on specific interactions. For instance, some users tried to click, instead of drag the orbs on the interface. As a result, we reconsidered the visual and textual prompts that users get on landing to better afford a dragging interaction.

Storyboarding and high-fiedility prototyping

I created high-fidelity screen mock-ups to illustrate the art direction and changes we learned from the paper prototype testing. Storyboards for users with different motivations (i.e. share a dream v.s. explore dreams) were created for us to better understand the interactions that could occur for different people.

Cognitive Walkthrough

We ran a cognitive walkthrough to examine the usability of each action for our concept. In each task that we wanted to test, the entire team went through each of these three questions for every action needed to carry out the task:

  1. Will correct action be sufficiently evident to the user?
  2. Will user notice that correct action is available?
  3. Will user associate and interpret system response?

It was apparent that there is insufficient affordance on the current interface for users to perform the correct actions. As an outcome, we had to introduce visual and textual cues of available actions.

Front-end and back-end implementation

I was responsible for developing the major functions of the front-end, including the interactions, styling, and connection with the back-end. I started by experimenting some functionality on JSFiddle. To ensure that everything integrates, I occasionally participated in the back-end development. Solving coding problem as a team was truly effective and educational.

Values and outcomes

This project reminded us of the importance of "form follows function". A web application does not always have to be designed with a dashboard or a card-based interface, nor does it have to align with the material design system.

As a designer, it is important to understand that interaction is a mean for users to access content or a function. We design interaction to echo and communicate.

In this project, our goal was to create an impactful and relatable way for creatives to interact with artworks on trove. To achieve this, we had to go beyond a card-based interface and reimagine ways to communicate the emotive and atmospheric qualities of a dream.

Another learning from this project was the fact that experiences can never be designed, but designed for. User experience is always subjective, it is an internal perception of a user based on external stimulus. We can only craft our design in a way which best (based on our understanding of the people) evoke such an experience.

In this project for instance, while some users enjoyed the atmospheric experience shaped by the background music of dreamer, some found it annoying. Some preferred a more realistic graphics style compared to the simple flat design we had. As designers, we have to acknowledge the different perspectives and to strive for a balance in our design decisions.
